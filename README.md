# The Broken Mirror: Representation Biases in Hugging Face’s Art Exhibition
Abstract: 
This article examines the Hugging Face art exhibition, a computer vision installation located at the company’s headquarters in Paris, which transforms images of passers-by in real time by placing them within historical artistic periods using a local AI model. Drawing on the theoretical framework of Crawford and Paglen (2021) and the literature on biases in facial recognition, we investigate the extent to which the system reproduces or challenges representational biases in its outputs. Through ethnographic observation and interviews with visitors, we identified a systematic pattern: although the system faithfully renders the subjects’ clothing and silhouette, it consistently abstracts facial features, producing white, male figures of European appearance, regardless of the actual demographics of the person in front of the screen. We interpret this result as the unintended consequence of two overlapping design decisions: (1) an anonymisation module aimed at protecting privacy, and (2) a training corpus centred on the Western European artistic canon. We conclude that the neutrality to which the system aspires is not such, because by attempting not to represent anyone in particular, it ends up always reproducing the same imagined subject, re-centring whiteness and masculinity as the implicit norm of art history.

# Keywords: facial recognition, art, qualitative analysis, bias, representation

# 1. Introduction

The proliferation of artificial intelligence in everyday life has brought with it a renewed urgency to interrogate who these systems see, how they see, and on whose terms. Facial recognition, generative imagery, and real-time transformation tools are no longer confined to research laboratories or state surveillance apparatuses; they now appear in galleries, public squares, and cultural events, inviting ordinary people into encounters with algorithmic classification. Yet the migration of these technologies into aesthetic and participatory contexts does not neutralize the political stakes embedded in their design. If anything, it renders those stakes more visible, and more worthy of sustained critical attention.

This paper takes as its object of study the Hugging Face AI Art Exhibition, an ephemeral visual computing installation situated at the company's Paris headquarters on the Rue du Caire. The installation uses an augmented camera to capture and transform the video feed of passersby in real time, inserting them into historical art periods through a locally run AI model. Operating under a privacy-by-design protocol, it processes footage without storing or transmitting identifiable data. The result is an encounter that is simultaneously aesthetic and classificatory: the system must interpret a human face and assign it a visual and historical identity. In doing so, it inevitably performs a series of choices about whose faces fit which periods, which visual conventions define a given era, and what it means to "represent" a person through the lens of art history.

These choices are never neutral. Drawing on Crawford and Paglen's (2021) framework in Excavating AI, we treat the exhibition not merely as a creative interface but as a repository of cultural and political classifications that require excavation. Crawford and Paglen's central argument is that machine learning systems do not passively reflect the world; they actively construct versions of it through the categories, labels, and images used to train them. Applied to an art exhibition, this insight raises a set of questions that go beyond technical performance: Who gets to decide what an image means? What kinds of identities are made legible by the system, and which are distorted, flattened, or erased? Can the visual conventions of European art history, which form the implied reference point of the installation's aesthetic transformations, accommodate the full diversity of faces that stand before it?

These questions are sharpened by decades of scholarship on bias in AI. Research has consistently shown that facial recognition and classification systems perform unevenly across demographic groups, producing higher error rates for darker-skinned individuals, women, and those who fall outside the normative categories encoded in training data (Buolamwini & Gebru, 2018; Raji et al., 2020). Crucially, these disparities are not incidental technical failures but structural outcomes of choices made about data collection, labeling, and evaluation. As Noble (2018) argues in the context of search engines, such systems do not merely reflect social inequalities, they actively reproduce and legitimize them. The Hugging Face exhibition, as a public and participatory application of similar underlying logics, offers a compelling site for examining whether these dynamics persist when AI is deployed in the name of art and democratization.

Our guiding research question is therefore to what extent and in what ways does the AI system in the Hugging Face Art Exhibition reproduce or challenge representational biases in its outputs? To address this question, we adopt a mixed-methods design anchored in qualitative inquiry. Our methodology combines semi-structured interviews with a developer involved in the project, multimodal fieldwork conducted over one or two full days at the exhibition, and intercept interviews with visitors gathered on-site. This triangulated approach allows us to analyze the exhibition from three complementary vantage points: the intentions and design choices embedded in the system's development, the observable patterns in the system's outputs as they unfold in real time, and the interpretive experiences of the people who interact with it.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 situates the study within the relevant theoretical literature on facial recognition, algorithmic bias, and the politics of representation in both AI and the arts. Section 3 elaborates our research question and its rationale. Section 4 describes the case study in greater detail. Section 5 outlines our methodology. Sections 6 and 7 present and discuss our findings. Section 8 offers conclusions, and Section 9 reflects on the limitations of the study.


# 2. Theoretical framework

# 2.1 How facial recognition works
At a general level, facial recognition systems operate through a sequence of computational steps. First, the system detects a face in an image or video frame. Next, it normalizes the image by aligning facial features such as the eyes, nose, and mouth. Then, a machine-learning model extracts a numerical representation, often called an embedding, which captures patterns the system has learned to associate with faces. Finally, this vector is compared with stored templates in order to verify identity or search for a match (El Fadel, 2025). In contemporary systems, deep neural networks are typically used because they are highly effective at learning complex visual patterns from large datasets.

Although this process appears technical and objective, it depends heavily on human decisions made long before deployment. Designers choose what kinds of faces to collect, how to label them, which features matter, and what counts as a successful match. These choices shape the model’s understanding of the face as an object of analysis. As a result, facial recognition does not simply “discover” identity; it constructs a computational version of identity through training data and classification logic (Crawford & Paglen, 2021). This construction becomes especially significant when the system is applied to socially sensitive contexts such as gender, race, and criminal suspicion.

# 2.2 Sources of bias
Bias in facial recognition emerges from multiple stages of the pipeline. One major source is the training dataset. If the dataset contains more images of certain demographic groups than others, the resulting model will tend to perform better for those groups. This is not only a matter of quantity but also of diversity in lighting, pose, expression, age, skin tone, and camera conditions. Buolamwini and Gebru (2018) demonstrated that commercial gender-classification systems performed significantly worse on darker-skinned women than on lighter-skinned men, revealing that accuracy is not evenly distributed across populations.
Another important source of bias is labeling. Facial-recognition datasets often rely on simplified or externally imposed categories such as race, age, and gender. These labels may be treated as if they were natural and self-evident, but in practice they reflect social conventions and institutional assumptions. Crawford and Paglen (2021) argue that such classification systems are political because they define what kinds of differences are visible and meaningful to the machine. Their analysis shows that training datasets do not merely contain images; they contain assumptions about who people are, how they should be grouped, and what visual traits are taken to stand for identity.

Bias can also be introduced during model evaluation and deployment. A system may perform well in a benchmark test but fail in real-world settings because the conditions of use differ from those in the lab. In practice, facial-recognition tools are often deployed in environments marked by unequal power relations, such as policing, border security, and workplace surveillance. In these settings, even small error rates can have serious consequences, especially when errors are unevenly distributed across racialized or marginalized groups (Raji et al., 2020). Thus, facial-recognition bias is not just a technical limitation; it is a social issue with material effects.

# 2.3 Racial and Gender Bias
One of the most widely discussed forms of bias in facial recognition concerns race. Numerous studies have found that these systems tend to misidentify darker-skinned individuals at higher rates than lighter-skinned individuals, particularly when the datasets used to train the models are not sufficiently representative (Bacchini et al., 2019). These disparities are often linked to the historical underrepresentation of racial minorities in image datasets and to the use of categories that flatten complex identities into broad groups. The consequence is that some populations are systematically more visible to the system than others, not because of inherent facial differences, but because of unequal data practices and institutional design.

Gender classification is also deeply problematic. Many facial-recognition systems operate with binary gender categories, even though gender identity is not binary in social reality. This produces both conceptual and practical distortions, since the system is trained to infer gender from appearance alone. As a result, the model can reinforce stereotypes about what male and female faces are supposed to look like. This issue matters not only because of accuracy, but because it turns gender into a visible trait that can be guessed, sorted, and monitored by an external observer. In this sense, facial recognition often reproduces normativity rather than reflecting identity.

# 2.4 Why Bias Matters
The importance of facial-recognition bias extends far beyond technical performance. These systems are now used in contexts where a mistaken identification can lead to exclusion, suspicion, or punishment. If facial recognition is less accurate for certain groups, then those groups bear a disproportionate share of the system’s harms. This creates an unequal distribution of risk that mirrors broader patterns of social inequality. Facial recognition therefore should be understood as part of a larger political economy of surveillance and classification, rather than as a simple image-processing tool.

This perspective is especially relevant to your project because the Hugging Face AI Art exhibition also involves questions of interpretation and representation. If an AI system produces different outputs depending on how subjects are labeled or described, then bias is not only a matter of error but also of meaning. Who gets to decide what an image means? What kinds of identities are made legible? Which features are emphasized, ignored, or distorted? These are not only technical questions but also theoretical ones, and facial recognition offers a strong foundation for addressing them.

# 2.5 The question of representation in the arts and how it relates to our case
The concept of representation in the arts involves a process of mediation and selection. In the social sciences, representation is, above all, an exercise of authority over who or what is worthy of being made visible. When talking about art, this representation shifts towards classification, as referring to a piece as art is very abstract, within the field of art categorises it according to predefined labels and periods.
Classification is an exercise of power. From the perspective of the sociology of culture, classifying art into periods (e.g., Renaissance, Baroque, Impressionism) is a historical construct of European academic institutions. Bowker and Leigh Star (1999) argue that all classification is invisible and deeply political, the categories we choose to organise the world determine what is included and what is excluded. 
In a similar way, AI also works with labels, with the difference that labels in AI models serve a purpose under a programming language but also in reality, influencing human sensemaking in relation with the machine that works with those labels. In the case of art, labels only exist in the discourse surrounding art pieces, as the art piece itself is defined by its singularity.

# 3. Case Study: Hugging Face Art Exhibition

# 3.1 The Company Behind the Exhibition
Hugging Face was founded in 2016 by French entrepreneurs Clément Delangue, Julein Chaumond and Thomas Wolf, the initial goal was to build an AI powered chatbot for teenagers before pivoting and stating toward their final activity which is an open source machine learning platform. The turning point came in 2018 with the release of Transformers, an open-source library that provided broad access to pre-trained AI models for natural language processing (NLP), significantly lowering the barrier to entry for developers and researchers worldwide.
 
As of today, Hugging Face’s stated mission is to “democratize good machine learning and maximize its positive impact across industries and society” positioning itself as the equivalent of GitHub for the machine learning community. Basically as a centralized repository where researchers, data scientists and engineers can share and build upon each other’s work. In 2025, the platform hosts over 1.5 million models, 300,000 datasets, and counts more than 13 million users, having established itself as the default infrastructure for sharing and discovering open-source AI models. However, the platform’s openness also raises significant ethical challenges. Hugging face’s own Ethics team acknowledges that machine learning models, as sociotechnical systems, amplify social trends that may exacerbate inequities and harmful biases in ways that depend on their deployment context and are constantly evolving.

# 3.2 The exhibition
The Hugging Face art exhibition is an ephemeral visual computing installation that uses an augmented camera to capture and transform the video feed of passersby in real time through a local AI model and insert people into an historical art period. Technically, the system operates under a “privacy by design” protocol (See Figure 1), where processing is performed internally without connecting to external servers as detailed in their privacy statement (Hugging Face, 2025), ensuring that the captured video is immediately distorted to prevent subject identification and instantly discarded without leaving any persistent records or databases. The installation not only seeks to generate a highly abstract aesthetic piece disconnected from the user’s actual appearance.

# 4. Methodology

# 4.1 Study Design
To investigate how the AI system in the Hugging Face exhibition reproduces or challenges representational biases, we propose a mixed-methods research design with a predominantly qualitative approach. Our methodology is grounded in the framework proposed by Crawford and Paglen (2021), treating the exhibition not merely as a technical interface, but as a repository of cultural and political classifications that require ‘excavation’.

# 4.2 Fieldwork
Secondly, we conducted fieldwork over one full day at the exhibition, adopting a multimodal ethnographic approach (Dicks et al., 2011) that combines participant observation with semi-structured interviews. This phase is divided into two components:
The first part consists of the documentation of the performance, utilizing what Pink (2025) defines as sensory ethnography. This involves a photographic and descriptive recording of users’ interactions with the AI to analyze the "materiality" of the digital encounter and how the system responds to various inputs in real-time. This method allows us to capture the "social life" of the algorithm (Ziewitz, 2016) within the specific context of the gallery. The second part involves intercept interviews with visitors, guided by an instrument developed by the authors (see our Github). This technique is designed to elicit immediate reflections on the "algorithmic experience" (Alvarado et al., 2022), focusing on how users interpret the outputs and the potential biases they perceive during the interaction.

# 4.3 Participants
Our fieldwork involved intercept interviews with 15 participants recruited on-site as they passed in front of the installation (as seen in Figure 2). The sample was demographically broad, spanning an age range of approximately 15 to 60 years old and drawing from diverse socioeconomic backgrounds, though it was not designed to be statistically representative.
The general reception of the exhibition among participants was one of curiosity and amusement. Most interviewees engaged with the installation in a lighthearted manner and did not spontaneously reflect on its underlying mechanisms or implications. A recurring pattern across interviews was a reluctance to develop sustained critical commentary, and none of the participants ultimately elaborated at length on their impressions when invited to do so.
# 5. Results
In the headquarters of Hugging Face, at 36 Rue du Caire, there is a big screen facing the street at the left side of the main entrance. At the two sides of the screen, there are two sheets detailing the content of the exhibition with a QR code containing the privacy policy of the installation which states that the exhibition is GDPR compliant and stores no data from the pedestrians.

Then, we started to observe the exhibition and how it is made and which patterns can be observed. We concluded that it consists of a rotation of a selection of era or style (≈15 different artistic eras/styles has been observed) that switches every three seconds based on the video flow the webcam at the top of the screen provides. We noted as well that the flow is not reactive enough to represent passing by pedestrians, you need to stand there for several seconds for the AI to process your appearance and show it back on the screen. Then, we started stopping people to gather their opinions, present the exhibition, and harvest their impressions and reflections on the screen. 

Through sustained observation of the installation, we were able to map out its basic operating logic. The system cycles through a rotating selection of approximately fifteen distinct artistic eras and styles, ranging from periods such as the Renaissance and Baroque to more recent stylistic movements, switching between them at roughly three-second intervals. The transitions are driven by the live video feed captured by a webcam mounted at the top of the screen.
 
One significant technical observation concerns the system's temporal responsiveness. The AI does not process movement in real time with sufficient fidelity to capture passersby in motion. A person must remain stationary in front of the screen for several seconds before the system registers their presence and produces a transformed output. This latency effectively selects for a particular kind of interaction: deliberate, voluntary, and sustained engagement, rather than incidental exposure. The exhibition is not passively experienced; it demands a conscious decision to stop and stand.
 
The most analytically significant finding to emerge from our fieldwork concerns the system's approach to rendering human subjects. Through repeated observation of different individuals standing before the screen, varying in apparent gender, skin tone, age, and dress, we identified a consistent and reproducible pattern in the outputs.
 
The system demonstrates a high degree of fidelity when reproducing non-facial bodily elements: clothing, bags, silhouettes, and postures were all rendered with relative accuracy and stylistic coherence within the given artistic period. However, when it came to facial features and exposed skin, such as the hands, neck, and face, the system's output was systematically abstract and sketch-like, losing the specificity of the subject's actual appearance. In place of this specificity, the rendered faces consistently presented as those of white, apparently male, European figures, regardless of the demographic characteristics of the person actually standing before the screen.
 
We interpret this pattern as the signature of an anonymization module embedded within the pipeline, a deliberate technical choice designed to prevent the system from producing identifiable representations of real individuals, in keeping with its stated privacy-by-design protocol. Understood as a privacy measure, this design decision is legitimate and coherent with the ethical commitments of the project. However, its representational consequences are far from neutral.
 
By abstracting away all individual facial characteristics and defaulting to a white, male, European aesthetic norm, the system effectively erases the ethnic, gender, and social diversity of the people who stand before it. Rather than inserting a Black woman, a person of East Asian descent, or a visibly working-class individual into the visual language of art history, the system produces a generic, unmarked subject that, within the conventions of European art history, defaults to whiteness and maleness as the implicit standard.
 
This finding resonates directly with Crawford and Paglen's (2021) argument that machine learning systems do not passively reflect the world but actively construct versions of it. The construction here is not incidental but structural: the very mechanism intended to protect individual privacy reproduces, at scale, the representational exclusions that have historically marginalized non-white and non-male subjects from the Western artistic canon. In attempting to represent no one in particular, the system ends up representing the same person every time.
 
This raises a broader concern regarding historical representation. If the visual rendering of historical art periods is systematically whitewashed, not through explicit intent but through the compounding logic of anonymization defaults and training data conventions, the installation risks re-centering European whiteness as the universal subject of art history. As Bowker and Leigh Star (1999) argue, classification systems determine what is included and what is excluded, and the default outputs of this installation are no exception to that logic.
 
# 6. Discussion of the results
The central finding of our fieldwork, that the installation consistently renders human subjects as white, apparently male, European figures regardless of who actually stands before the screen, demands more than a technical explanation. It demands a sociological one.

The designers of the exhibition faced a genuine dilemma. Facial recognition and generative AI systems are, as we saw, sites of racial and gender bias. Producing realistic renderings of real individuals' faces risked reproducing those biases in visible and potentially offensive ways: a black woman misrendered as a white woman, a person of East Asian descent flattened into an ill-fitting European aesthetic template. The anonymization module appears to have been conceived, at least in part, as a way out of this problem. If the system renders no one's face accurately, then no one's face can be misrepresented.

This logic is not unreasonable. But it rests on a deeply flawed premise that abstraction is neutral. What the results show is that abstraction is never truly featureless. Every act of generalization defaults to something, and what this system defaults to is the unmarked subject of European art history: implicitly white, implicitly male, implicitly normative. The attempt to sidestep the problem of representational bias does not dissolve that bias; it displaces it, and in doing so, institutionalizes it.

This is, in essence, a paradox of avoidance. By refusing to make explicit category choices the system makes the most consequential category choice of all. It elects whiteness and maleness as the default condition of the human subject in art history. What presents itself as a non-decision is, structurally, a very specific decision: to treat the white male European face as the baseline from which all other faces deviate, and therefore as the safest face to show when the system does not want to commit.

Crawford and Paglen's (2021) insight that machine learning systems actively construct versions of the world rather than passively reflecting it is fully borne out here. The construction, however, is not located in a classification error or a biased label. It is located in the design of the safety mechanism itself. The system was engineered to protect users from misrepresentation, and in doing so it misrepresents all of them, uniformly, in the same direction: toward the center of the Western canon.

This has consequences that exceed the individual encounter. The exhibition is situated in a public space, facing the street, visible to a demographically diverse urban population. Each time a person of color, a woman, or anyone whose appearance falls outside the narrow aesthetic conventions of European portraiture stands before the screen and sees a white male face rendered in their place, the installation does not merely fail to represent them. It actively replaces them. It tells them, at the level of the image, that the universal subject of art history looks like someone else.
Yet the paradox of avoidance described above does not emerge in a vacuum. It is enabled, upstream, by a prior curatorial decision that has received insufficient scrutiny: the selection of artistic styles available to the system. Based on the outputs observed, the system appears to draw predominantly from the Western European canon — the Baroque, the Renaissance, Romanticism, Impressionism. If this is the case, these traditions developed within specific historical conditions of patronage and portraiture whose visual conventions were built around a particular vision of the human subject: white, European, and predominantly male. A training corpus weighted in this direction would encode assumptions about which artistic traditions are legible, valuable, and universal — assumptions that compound, rather than cause, the anonymization effects described above. 
The representational bias observed in the output is therefore overdetermined, produced by two compounding decisions rather than one. Even if the privacy mechanism were redesigned to render faces with greater fidelity, every subject would still be routed through an aesthetic framework constructed for someone else. A dataset that included Yoruba court art, Mughal miniature portraiture, Chinese ink painting, or Andean textile iconography would not only expand the visual vocabulary of the installation — it would shift the gravitational center of the system's defaults. The current impasse, in which every visitor is universalized into whiteness, is in part a labeling and curation problem: the training corpus was assembled as though European art history were synonymous with art history. Foucault's (1991) concept of disciplinary power is instructive here — the installation does not coerce, it classifies, and classification always proceeds from a norm. The choice of which styles populate that norm is the choice of which bodies, faces, and visual traditions are treated as the baseline of human representation.
This connects to a broader argument about why representational stakes in AI systems matter beyond the aesthetic. As hooks (1992) and Mirzoeff (2011) have argued, the capacity to see oneself reflected in cultural production is constitutive of identity, not incidental to it. To be admitted into the frame of art history only by having one's face replaced with a canonical European one is to be told that one's presence requires translation before it can be aestheticized. A more equitable design would ask, from the outset, whose art counts as training data — and build the dataset accordingly.
Noble's (2018) argument that algorithmic systems do not merely reflect social inequalities but actively reproduce and legitimize them applies with particular force here. The exhibition positions itself as a democratizing gesture. An invitation for anyone passing by to enter the frame of art history. But the frame, as it turns out, has a fixed center of gravity. It accommodates diverse bodies, diverse clothing, diverse silhouettes. It does not accommodate diverse faces. The democratic promise of the installation is undercut by the representational architecture of its privacy solution.

It is also worth noting the asymmetry this creates within the output itself. The system renders clothing, posture, and accessories with relative stylistic fidelity to the artistic period being displayed. A visitor's coat becomes a Renaissance doublet; their bag becomes a Baroque still-life prop. These bodily elements are transformed but preserved. The face, by contrast, is not transformed, it is replaced. The result is an uncanny composite: a historically styled body inhabited by a face that belongs to no one present, and to one specific demographic default. The body is individualized; the face is universalized. And the universal, here, is white.

# 7. Conclusions
This study set out to investigate the extent to which the Hugging Face AI Art Exhibition reproduces or challenges representational biases in its outputs. Our answer, based on fieldwork, observation, and close analysis of the system's behavior, is unambiguous: the installation reproduces bias, not despite its ethical intentions but, in part, because of them.
The system's anonymization protocol, designed to protect user privacy and to avoid the well-documented risks of biased facial rendering, produces a default output that re-centers the white, male, European subject as the universal face of art history. By attempting to represent no one in particular, it ends up representing the same imagined person every time. The neutrality it aspires to is not neutral at all; it is a specific aesthetic and political position that mistakes the dominant for the universal.

This finding has implications that extend beyond the Hugging Face installation. It speaks to a broader tension in the design of AI systems that are intended to be ethical, inclusive, and safe. The instinct to avoid explicit classification is understandable. But abstraction is not an exemption. Every system must output something, and whatever it outputs reflects a set of assumptions about what the default, the norm, and the baseline look like. When those assumptions are not interrogated, they tend to reproduce the dominant conventions of the culture in which the system was built.

The Hugging Face exhibition is not a bad-faith project. The company's stated commitment to open-source development, ethical AI, and privacy-by-design reflects genuine and admirable values. But good intentions do not guarantee equitable outcomes, and this case illustrates why. The lesson is not that privacy protection should be abandoned, but that the design of those protections must itself be subject to critical scrutiny.
 
# 8. Limitations 
The most significant limitation of this study is that we had no access to the underlying architecture of the system itself. The algorithm used to generate the artistic renderings, the training dataset on which it was built, the parameters of the anonymization module, and the curatorial decisions that shaped the selection of artistic periods all remained opaque to us. Our analysis is therefore necessarily inferential: we observed the outputs of the system and reasoned backward toward their causes, but we cannot confirm the technical mechanisms responsible for the patterns we identified. 

This gap is compounded by our inability to conduct the planned interview with one of the system's developers before finalizing the paper. A conversation with someone involved in building the installation would either corroborate or complicate our interpretation, and might surface design constraints, intentions, or trade-offs that fieldwork alone cannot access. 
Finally, our analysis is shaped by a theoretical framework  that is explicitly critical of power structures embedded in classification systems. This means our reading of the installation was oriented from the outset toward identifying exclusion and bias. A researcher approaching the same exhibition from, say, a human-computer interaction perspective, or one more focused on the technical achievements of privacy-by-design, might draw substantially different conclusions from the same observations. We do not consider this a flaw, but it is a boundary the reader should keep in mind when evaluating our findings.
